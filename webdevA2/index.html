The Solar System

The Solar System

The Solar System consists of the Sun and all the celestial objects gravitationally bound to it, including eight planets, dwarf planets, moons, asteroids, and comets. It formed about 4.5 billion years ago from a cloud of gas and dust, and is located in the Milky Way galaxy. The Sun, a star, is the central body, and the planets orbit it in elliptical paths.

The Solar System in the Universe

The Solar System in the Universe

The Solar System is just one of hundreds of billions nestled within the Milky Way galaxy, a vast spiral system spanning about 100,000 light-years. Our Sun, and everything bound to it, resides in a relatively quiet region known as the Orion Arm, a minor spiral arm located about 27,000 light-years from the galactic center. Beyond the Milky Way, we're part of a collection of galaxies called the Local Group, which includes Andromeda and dozens of smaller galaxies. While the Solar System may seem vast on a human scale, it's just a tiny speck in a cosmic ocean. Understanding where we sit in this larger structure helps put into perspective just how expansive, and mysterious the universe truly is.

Life Cycle Of Stars

Life Cycle Of Stars

Stars are born in nebulae, evolve through a main sequence phase, and eventually exhaust their fuel, leading to different fates depending on their mass. Low to medium mass stars become red giants, then planetary nebulae, and finally white dwarfs. Massive stars become red supergiants, explode as supernovae, and then collapse into either neutron stars or black holes.

Apollo 11

Apollo 11

Apollo 11 was the first crewed mission to land humans on the Moon. Launched on July 16, 1969, the mission culminated in Neil Armstrong and Buzz Aldrin becoming the first humans to walk on the lunar surface on July 20, 1969. Michael Collins piloted the Command Module Columbia in lunar orbit while Armstrong and Aldrin landed in the Lunar Module Eagle.

Voyager 1 & 2

Voyager 1 & 2

Voyager 1 and Voyager 2 are twin spacecraft launched by NASA in 1977 to explore the outer solar system and beyond. They are the most distant human-made objects from Earth and are currently exploring interstellar space. Both spacecraft have far exceeded their original five-year mission, continuing to transmit valuable scientific data from interstellar space.

Hubble Space Telescope

Hubble Space Telescope

The Hubble Space Telescope is a large space-based observatory that has revolutionized astronomy by providing incredibly sharp images of space, free from the distortion of Earth's atmosphere. Launched in 1990, it orbits Earth at an altitude of about 340 miles and has captured stunning images of planets, stars, galaxies, and more, contributing significantly to our understanding of the universe.

James Webb Space Telescope

James Webb Space Telescope

The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is a large, infrared space telescope designed to observe the universe's earliest stars and galaxies, as well as exoplanets and objects within our solar system. Launched on December 25, 2021, it is the most powerful space telescope ever built, with a primary mirror more than six times the light-collecting area of the Hubble Space Telescope. JWST will study the universe's cosmic dawn, analyze exoplanet atmospheres, and explore the formation of stars and galaxies.
